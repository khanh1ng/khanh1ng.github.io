I trade gold discretionarily on the side, and I keep a running journal of every rule I break and what it cost. This post organizes those notes into something coherent. A caveat up front, because I'd raise it reading anyone else's version of this: these are **heuristics from a discretionary journal, not backtested signals**. The sample is one trader, the selection bias is real, and price-action frameworks are notoriously unfalsifiable — you can always reinterpret a chart after the fact. I keep the notes anyway, because the journaling itself is where the discipline comes from. Treat what follows as a record of process, not a claim of edge.

## The organizing idea: look for liquidity, not patterns

The single reframe that changed how I read a chart: **price moves toward liquidity, not toward patterns.** Chart patterns are descriptions; resting orders are mechanics. Large participants need size filled, and size gets filled where liquidity pools — clusters of stops above equal highs, below equal lows, around obvious levels every retail participant marks the same way.

Consequences of taking that seriously:

- A "support break" is often not a trend change; it's a stop hunt. Big players push price through an obvious level to trigger stop-losses and stop-sells, absorb that liquidity into their own orders, and reverse. The break *is* the liquidity event.
- If price pushes down and comes right back up, the read is not "buyers are strong" — it's "there wasn't enough liquidity down there to fill the move." Expect a second visit.
- Corollary I wrote down after paying for it: **don't enter on the first sweep of a level. Enter on the second or third.** The first touch is where the trap is set; by the second or third move into the zone you can see whether the liquidity was absorbed or the level is genuinely defended.

## Structure before zones, zones before entries

The workflow that keeps me out of most bad trades, in order:

1. **Market structure on 4h/daily first.** Trend, swing highs/lows, who's in control. Everything below inherits from this — a demand zone in a bearish structure is not the same trade as the identical zone in a bullish one.
2. **Points of interest: supply and demand zones.** Order blocks where prior imbalance originated. The critical classification is **strong vs. weak**, and strength is determined on the *higher* timeframe, not the timeframe you entered from. A test I use: did this zone cause another zone to fail? If a supply zone absorbed every bid in a demand zone below it, that's information — the buys are spent, the bias is bearish.
3. **Liquidity as the entry trigger.** Trade from strong zones toward weak ones, and only after liquidity has been swept or visibly accumulated. Structure says *what*, zones say *where*, liquidity says *when*.

## Discipline rules, learned expensively

- **Being early is exactly as bad as being wrong.** The P&L can't tell the difference. My worst habit is seeing the destination correctly and entering before the market has finished hunting stops on the way there. The fix is boring: wait for the reversal to confirm, even when the move "must" come — especially then, because a straight drop with no confirmation is precisely how it looks right before it isn't one.
- **Don't call a fakeout without confirmation.** Suspecting a fakeout is a hypothesis, not a position.
- **The job is not finding A+ setups. It's ignoring everything that isn't one.** Most of the edge in discretionary trading is subtraction.
- **Risk 1% per trade, no exceptions.** Not because 1% is magic, but because it makes any single trade's outcome irrelevant, which is the only mental state in which the rules above are followable. The journal is unambiguous on this: every entry that violated the 1% rule correlates with violating at least one other rule on the same trade. Oversizing isn't a sizing error; it's a symptom.
- **Stop placement is a liquidity question too.** An SL at the obvious level is a donation — it sits inside the exact pool that gets swept. Stops go beyond the liquidity pool or the trade doesn't get taken. Still the weakest part of my execution, per the journal's loss attribution.

## One meta-note

"Free information — you are the product." Written in the journal after a week of following signal channels. Any trade idea that arrives free, at scale, to thousands of people simultaneously, is either noise or exit liquidity for whoever published it. The corollary applies to this post: nothing here is a signal. It's a process document, and the process is mostly about not trading.
